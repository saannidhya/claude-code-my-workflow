# Literature Review: Intra-Family Housing Transfers, Inheritance, and the Supply of Homes

**Date:** 2026-06-09
**Project:** 03_family_homes
**Query:** Clusters: intergenerational housing transfers; property-tax lock-in (Prop 13/19); transfer-tax timing; housing misallocation; turnover lock-in; filtering; estate/gift timing; Prop 19 institutional.

## Summary

Three mature literatures surround — but do not occupy — the question this paper asks.
First, the *bequest* literature (Bernheim-Shleifer-Summers 1985; McGarry 1999; Kopczuk
2007; Piketty 2011) studies why and when wealth passes between generations, almost
entirely with survey or estate-tax data in which houses are dollar values, not parcels.
Second, the *lock-in* literature (Wasi & White 2005; Ferreira 2010; Fonseca & Liu 2024;
Coven et al. 2024) shows that taxes and financing lock owners in place, but its unit is
the living owner's tenure spell — it stops at death. Third, the *transfer-tax timing*
literature (Kopczuk & Munroe 2015; Slemrod, Weber & Shan 2017; Best & Kleven 2018;
Hilber & Lyytikäinen 2017) establishes that real-estate transactions respond sharply,
and mostly through timing and volume, to tax notches — but studies market transactions.

What none of these provide: a national, deeds-level accounting of the *non-market*
channel — homes that change hands inside families, at zero price, off the MLS, outside
every price index — and causal evidence on whether tax policy keeps those homes off
the market. The closest neighbors are an industry note (Delventhal/Cotality 2026:
~340k US homes transferred via inheritance in the 12 months ending Aug 2025, ~7% of
all transfers), the heirs'-property estimate of Dobbs & Johnson Gaither (2023, ~444k
parcels), and California's LAO (2017), which documented 60,000–80,000 parent-child
excluded transfers per year in CA and observed that the exclusion "appears to be
encouraging children to hold on to their parents' homes to use as rentals or other
purposes instead of putting them on the for sale market." That LAO observation is,
in effect, our paper's hypothesis — stated by a fiscal agency, never tested causally.

## Key papers (selection)

### Glaeser & Luttmer (2003 AER 93(4):1027–1046) — Misallocation of Housing under Rent Control
- **Contribution:** Welfare loss from allocating housing by queue rather than price.
- **Relevance:** Our welfare framing — allocation by *birth* rather than price is the
  family analogue of allocation by queue.

### Ferreira (2010 JPubE 94:661–673) — Prop 13 lock-in
- **Method:** RD-style comparison around age-55 portability (Props 60/90).
- **Finding:** Tax-basis portability significantly raises moving propensity.
- **Relevance:** Same institution (CA acquisition-value assessment); we study its
  *intergenerational* margin.

### Wasi & White (2005 BWPUA:59–97) — Prop 13 tenure lengthening
- **Finding:** CA owner tenure +1.04 years vs comparison states (1970–2000).

### Kopczuk & Munroe (2015 AEJ:EP 7(2):214–257); Slemrod, Weber & Shan (2017 JUE 100:137–153); Best & Kleven (2018 REStud 85(1):157–193); Hilber & Lyytikäinen (2017 JUE 101:57–73)
- **Findings:** Sharp timing/bunching and volume responses of property transactions to
  transfer-tax notches (NY/NJ mansion tax; DC notch; UK stamp duty).
- **Relevance:** Methodological template for the Prop 19 deadline bunching design; and
  benchmark elasticities for market transactions to compare with our non-market ones.

### Bernheim, Shleifer & Summers (1985 JPE 93(6):1045–1076); McGarry (1999 JPubE 73(3):321–351); Kopczuk (2007 QJE 122(4):1801–1854); Piketty (2011 QJE 126(3):1071–1131)
- **Relevance:** Bequest motives, inter-vivos vs at-death timing (tax-sensitive), and
  the macro return of inheritance. We give this literature its missing *asset-level*
  counterpart for housing.

### Fonseca & Liu (2024 JF 79:3729–3772) — Mortgage rate lock-in
- **Relevance:** The other big modern lock-in; distinguishes our channel (the family/tax
  lock persists across generations, not just across rate cycles).

### Coven, Golder, Gupta & Ndiaye (2024, CEPR DP19230) — Property taxes and housing allocation
- **Relevance:** Structural OLG treatment of Prop 13 misallocation; cites no
  intergenerational deed-level evidence — our facts are complementary inputs.

### Dobbs & Johnson Gaither (2023 JRSS 38(1)) — heirs'-property extent (LightBox data); Burnett & Winters-Michaud (2025 ARER 54(3):617–634) — heirs'-property relative changes
- **Relevance:** Closest measurement exercises; cover only the heirs'-property subset.
- **Correction (claim-verifier):** earlier draft conflated these two papers; both now cited correctly.

### Glaeser & Gyourko (2005 JPE 113(2):345–375) — Urban decline and durable housing
- **Relevance:** Durability + slow exit shapes where family-held homes accumulate.

### Rosenthal (2014 AER 104(2):687–706) — Filtering
- **Relevance:** Family retention interrupts filtering — a supply channel for
  lower-income buyers that our counterfactual speaks to.

### Institutional: LAO (2017), LAO (2020 ballot analysis), CA BOE Prop 19 guidance, Delventhal/Cotality (2026)
- Prop 19 mechanics: passed Nov 3, 2020; parent-child exclusion narrowed effective
  Feb 16, 2021 (principal residence of transferor AND transferee, value cap = taxable
  value + $1M inflation-adjusted, family-farm carve-out; non-primary-residence
  exclusion eliminated). Portability provisions effective Apr 1, 2021.
- Documented pre-deadline rush (e.g., Sonoma County: 917 parent-child filings through
  Feb 5, 2021 vs ~193 typical; LA County backlog ~20,400 claims).

## Gaps and opportunities

1. **No national accounting of non-market transfers.** (To our knowledge; nearest:
   Cotality 2026 industry note, heirs'-property subset, CA-only LAO data.)
2. **No post-transfer trajectory evidence**: what happens to inherited/gifted homes —
   return to market, rental conversion, absentee holding — is undocumented at scale.
3. **No causal evaluation of Prop 19's intergenerational provisions** (to our
   knowledge as of 2026-06; re-sweep before submission).
4. The bequest-timing literature (inter-vivos vs at-death) has never been tested on
   the *housing* margin with deeds.
